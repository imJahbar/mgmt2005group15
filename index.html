What is Cybersecurity?

Understanding the basics

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, programs, and data from digital attacks, damage, or unauthorised access. It encompasses technologies, processes, and controls designed to protect digital infrastructure from threats.

The CIA Triad is the cornerstone model of information security: Confidentiality (only authorised users access data), Integrity (data is accurate and unaltered), and Availability (systems are accessible when needed).
Who are the Threat Actors?

Understanding who launches cyberattacks and why

Nation-State Attackers

Government-sponsored groups targeting critical infrastructure, defence systems, and sensitive government data. They have significant resources and advanced capabilities (APTs).

Cybercriminals

Organised crime groups motivated by financial gain. They deploy ransomware, conduct fraud, steal financial data, and sell credentials on the dark web.

Hacktivists

Individuals or groups attacking organisations for ideological, political, or social reasons. Common tactics include website defacement and data leaks.

Insider Threats

Current or former employees who misuse access privileges — either intentionally (malicious insiders) or accidentally through negligence or poor security practices.

Script Kiddies

Unskilled individuals who use pre-made hacking tools and scripts without understanding them. Though unsophisticated, they can still cause significant disruption.

AI-Assisted Threats

Attackers increasingly use artificial intelligence to automate phishing, generate deepfakes, discover vulnerabilities, and craft more convincing social engineering attacks.

Types of Cyber Threats

The most common attack types organisations and individuals face today

Malware

Software intentionally designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorised access to systems. Includes viruses, worms, trojans, spyware, and keyloggers.

Phishing & Social Engineering

Manipulating people into revealing sensitive information or taking harmful actions by impersonating trusted entities via email, phone, or SMS.

Password Attacks

Attempts to crack or steal passwords through brute force, dictionary attacks, or using credentials leaked from previous data breaches.

Denial of Service (DoS / DDoS)

Flooding a system with traffic to make it unavailable. Distributed attacks use networks of compromised machines (botnets) to amplify the impact.

Man-in-the-Middle (MitM)

Secretly intercepting communications between two parties, allowing the attacker to eavesdrop on or alter data in transit without either party knowing.

Ransomware

Malware that encrypts a victim's files and demands payment for the decryption key. Modern attacks also threaten to publish stolen data if the ransom is not paid.

Identity Theft

Stealing personal information such as ID numbers, banking details, or login credentials to impersonate a victim for financial gain or fraud.

Data Breaches

Unauthorised access to confidential data held by an organisation. Breaches expose customer records, financial data, and trade secrets, often affecting thousands of people.

How to Protect Yourself

Core defences every individual and organisation should implement

Strong Passwords & MFA

Use unique, complex passwords for every account. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) wherever possible — it blocks over 99% of account compromise attacks.

Keep Software Updated

Apply security patches promptly. Most successful attacks exploit known vulnerabilities for which patches already exist. Enable automatic updates where possible.

Think Before You Click

Verify sender identity before opening attachments or clicking links. Hover over links to preview URLs. When in doubt, navigate directly to the website instead.

Secure Your Devices

Use screen locks, encrypt storage, and install reputable antivirus software. Avoid rooting or jailbreaking devices, which weakens built-in security protections.

Safe Browsing & VPN

Only visit HTTPS sites for sensitive transactions. Use a trusted VPN on public Wi-Fi. Be cautious about browser extensions — they can have broad data access.

Back Up Your Data

Follow the 3-2-1 backup rule. Store backups offline or in a separate account to ensure ransomware cannot encrypt them. Test your backups regularly.

Zero Trust Principle: "Never trust, always verify." Treat every access request as potentially hostile, regardless of whether it originates inside or outside the network perimeter.
